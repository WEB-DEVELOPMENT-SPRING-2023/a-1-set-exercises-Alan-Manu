Overview

"Breaking Bad" is an American television drama series created by Vince Gilligan. The show originally aired on AMC from 2008 to 2013, spanning five seasons and a total of 62 episodes. The series follows the story of Walter White, a struggling high school chemistry teacher who is diagnosed with lung cancer and turns to cooking and selling methamphetamine with his former student, Jesse Pinkman, in order to provide for his family.
The show has been highly acclaimed for its writing, acting, direction, and cinematography, and has won numerous awards, including 16 Primetime Emmy Awards. Bryan Cranston, who played Walter White, won four consecutive Emmy Awards for Outstanding Lead Actor in a Drama Series, and Aaron Paul, who played Jesse Pinkman, won three Primetime Emmy Awards for Outstanding Supporting Actor in a Drama Series.
In addition to its critical acclaim, "Breaking Bad" has also become a cultural phenomenon and has been widely discussed and analyzed in popular culture. The show's popularity led to the creation of a spin-off series, "Better Call Saul," which premiered in 2015 and focuses on the character of Saul Goodman, played by Bob Odenkirk, who was a supporting character in "Breaking Bad."
